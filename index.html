About

I am a PhD candidate specialising in human factors, digital health, and human–computer interaction. Through behavioural and empirical research, I examine how people adopt, evaluate, trust, and work with interactive systems across digital health and related contexts.

My broader goal is to understand how digital and AI-enabled technologies can be designed and evaluated so that they are not only technically capable, but also usable, acceptable, safe, and effective in practice.

Research Overview

Digital health intervention research

Implementation, usability, acceptability, and trial-based evaluation

My previous work has examined digital health interventions including AI-powered rehabilitation for knee osteoarthritis, chatbot-delivered mental health support, health apps, and self-monitoring technologies.

Human–AI interaction under uncertainty

Ongoing theoretical and empirical work

More recently, I have been investigating how uncertainty in AI outputs shapes attention, trust, control-related behaviours, and the evolving dynamics of human interaction with AI-enabled systems.

Evaluation in real-world settings

Health technologies in practice rather than in isolation

A recurring theme of my work is whether digital technologies can meaningfully support care and self-management once deployed in applied or clinically relevant contexts.
